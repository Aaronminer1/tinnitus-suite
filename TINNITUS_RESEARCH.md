# Tinnitus: Comprehensive Research Report

> **Sources:** NIDCD/NIH (updated May 2023), Mayo Clinic (updated Nov 2022), American Tinnitus Association  
> **Compiled:** March 1, 2026

---

## TL;DR

Tinnitus — phantom sound with no external source — affects **10–25% of adults globally** and has **no cure**. It is primarily a brain phenomenon triggered by hearing-system damage, not a disease itself. The most evidence-backed approaches combine sound therapy with behavioral/psychological therapy. No drug is FDA-approved specifically for tinnitus.

---

## 1. What It Is

| Property | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 10–25% of adults; most common service-related disability in veterans |
| **Chronic threshold** | 3+ months duration |
| **Types** | **Subjective** (patient only) — vast majority; **Objective** (doctor can hear it too) — rare, usually vascular |
| **Sounds** | Ringing, buzzing, roaring, hissing, clicking, humming, squealing — varies per person |
| **Pulsatile tinnitus** | Rhythmic, in sync with heartbeat — warrants urgent imaging (MRI/CT) |

---

## 2. Root Causes & Neurological Mechanism

### Leading Theory

Damage to cochlear hair cells alters the signals sent to the auditory cortex. The brain **generates phantom sound** to compensate for missing frequency input — analogous to phantom limb pain. Abnormal interactions between the auditory cortex and non-auditory regions (emotion, attention) amplify distress and habituation difficulty.

### Common Causes

| Cause | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Noise-induced hearing loss** | Most common — concerts, firearms, heavy machinery, earbuds at high volume |
| **Age-related hearing loss (presbycusis)** | Nerve fiber decline with age |
| **Ear infection / earwax blockage** | Reversible — removing the blockage can eliminate tinnitus entirely |
| **Head or neck injury** | Damages inner ear, auditory nerve, or auditory brain regions; typically one ear |
| **Ototoxic medications** | NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin), certain antibiotics, anti-cancer drugs, diuretics, antimalarials, antidepressants |

### Less Common Causes

- **Ménière's disease** — inner ear fluid disorder; also causes vertigo and hearing loss
- **TMJ disorders** — jaw joint dysfunction irritates adjacent ear structures
- **Acoustic neuroma** — benign tumor on auditory/balance cranial nerve
- **Otosclerosis** — abnormal middle ear bone growth; tends to run in families
- **Cardiovascular disorders** — atherosclerosis, hypertension; causes pulsatile type
- **Chronic conditions** — diabetes, thyroid disease, migraines, lupus, multiple sclerosis, anemia

### Risk Factors

Male sex · Advancing age · Tobacco/alcohol use · Obesity · Loud-noise occupations (factory, military, music) · Cardiovascular disease · History of head injury

## 3. Diagnosis

1. **Primary care exam** — check for earwax/infection; medication review
2. **Audiological exam** — pure-tone hearing test across 10 frequencies in both ears (audiogram)
3. **Movement exam** — jaw clenching, eye movement, neck movement can alter subjective tinnitus → helps isolate somatosensory component
4. **Sound characterization** (clinically useful):
   - Clicking → muscle spasms around the ear
   - Pulsing/whooshing → vascular origin
   - Low-pitched ring → Ménière's disease or otosclerosis
   - High-pitched ring → noise damage, hearing loss, or acoustic neuroma
5. **Imaging** (if pulsatile or unilateral) — MRI or CT scan
6. **Blood tests** — anemia, thyroid, vitamins, cardiovascular markers

## 4. Treatments — Evidence Ranked

### A. Address the Underlying Cause *(most effective when applicable)*

- Earwax removal → often eliminates tinnitus immediately
- Treat ear infection
- Stop or switch ototoxic medication
- Treat blood vessel condition (medication or surgery)
- **Hearing aids** — for hearing-loss-driven tinnitus; amplifying external sound reduces tinnitus salience

---

### B. Sound Therapy *(strong evidence, most widely used)*

**Mechanism:** Controlled sound exposure can reverse the neural reorganisation that causes tinnitus by stimulating underused auditory pathways and reducing hyperactivity at the tinnitus frequency.

| Device / Method | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **White / pink / brown noise machines** | Mask tinnitus; especially effective for sleep (tabletop or smartphone app) |
| **In-ear masking devices** | Like hearing aids but emit continuous low-level noise throughout the day |
| **Combination devices** | Hearing amplification + noise masking in one unit |
| **Notched noise therapy** | Broadband noise with a frequency notch at the patient's exact tinnitus pitch — reduces lateral inhibition at that frequency in the auditory cortex *(therapeutic, not just masking)* |
| **Notched music therapy (NMT)** | Personalised music with the tinnitus frequency removed; exploits lateral inhibition to downregulate hyperactive auditory neurons at the tinnitus pitch; neuroplastic effects shown in multiple studies |

> **Note:** Notched therapies require accurate identification of the tinnitus frequency first — exactly what the Tone Finder step in the app provides.

---

### C. Behavioural Therapies *(strong evidence — best long-term outcomes)*

| Therapy | Description | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)** | Identifies and restructures negative thought patterns about tinnitus; reduces distress and improves quality of life | **Strongest evidence** — multiple RCTs; available online |
| **Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT)** | Directive counselling (reclassify tinnitus as a neutral signal) combined with a wearable sound generator; 12–24 month programme | Strong; requires trained audiologist |
| **Acceptance & Commitment Therapy (ACT)** | Reduces suffering by accepting (rather than fighting) the sound's presence | Growing evidence base |
| **Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR)** | Reduces emotional reactivity and hypervigilance to tinnitus | Moderate evidence |

---

### D. Neuromodulation *(cutting-edge)*

| Method | Status |
|---|---|
| **Bimodal stimulation — Lenire device** | Combines acoustic tones with mild electrical stimulation of the tongue; multiple RCTs showing significant tinnitus reduction; **FDA cleared 2023** (first device of its kind) |
| **Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS) paired with tones** | VNS pulses timed to acoustic tones drives targeted plasticity in auditory cortex; FDA Breakthrough Device designation; late-stage clinical trials |
| **Repetitive TMS (rTMS)** | Non-invasive magnetic pulses to auditory cortex or DLPFC; mixed results — optimal coil placement and frequency of sessions still being studied |
| **Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS)** | Invasive; unexpected tinnitus reduction observed in DBS patients treated for movement disorders; early/experimental only — not routine for tinnitus |
| **Non-invasive cochlear electrical stimulation** | Research-stage; aims to suppress tinnitus without damaging residual acoustic hearing |

---

### E. Medications *(no FDA-approved drug for tinnitus)*

- **Antidepressants / anxiolytics** — prescribed to manage depression, anxiety, and sleep disruption that accompany tinnitus, not the tinnitus itself
- **Research compounds** — drugs targeting hyperactive central auditory neurons post-cochlear damage; second-generation molecules in development after animal model success
- **IV Lidocaine** — transiently suppresses tinnitus in some patients but impractical and risky for regular use
- **Supplements** (ginkgo biloba, zinc, melatonin, B vitamins) — widely advertised; **none proven effective** in quality RCTs

---

### F. Lifestyle & Self-Management

| Action | Why It Helps |
|---|---|
| Wear hearing protection | Prevents further cochlear damage and worsening |
| Cap headphone volume | Prevent noise-induced progression |
| Reduce alcohol, caffeine, nicotine | All affect blood flow and tinnitus severity |
| White noise at bedtime | Reduces the contrast that makes tinnitus most audible at night |
| Regular cardiovascular exercise | Improves vascular health → reduces vascular-driven tinnitus |
| Stress management (biofeedback, yoga, exercise) | Stress is a reliable tinnitus amplifier |

## 5. Comorbidities & Quality-of-Life Impact

Tinnitus rarely exists in isolation. It strongly co-occurs with:

- **Hearing loss** — most patients have some measurable degree
- **Anxiety & depression** — bidirectional: tinnitus causes both, and both amplify tinnitus perception
- **Sleep disorders** — a quiet environment at night makes tinnitus most prominent
- **Cognitive difficulties** — concentration and memory impairment
- **Hyperacusis** — increased sound sensitivity, often co-occurring with tinnitus
- **Fatigue, headaches, social withdrawal**

Treating comorbidities significantly improves quality of life even when the tinnitus sound itself persists.

## 6. Frontier Research (2024–2026)

| Area | What's Happening |
|---|---|
| **Genetic risk mapping** | Large genomic databases analysed to identify risk alleles shared by tinnitus and age-related hearing loss — implications for screening and prevention |
| **Personalised tinnitus phenotyping** | Smartphone tracking + brain imaging (fMRI/EEG) used to characterise distinct tinnitus subtypes and match patients to the most effective treatment |
| **Zinc trapping** | 2024 University of Pittsburgh / NIH research: preventing noise-induced hearing loss by capturing excess zinc in cochlear hair cells immediately after noise exposure — a potential prophylactic intervention |
| **Glutamate antagonists** | Targeting cochlear excitotoxicity (excess glutamate release after noise trauma) as a tinnitus prevention strategy |
| **VNS + tone pairing** | Late-stage RCTs; if approved, would be the first neurostimulation drug-device combination specifically indicated for tinnitus |
| **Machine learning treatment matching** | EEG + patient-reported app data fed into ML models to predict individual treatment response before committing to months of therapy |

## 6.5 — Known Adverse Reactions & Contraindications

### Documented Side Effects of Sound Therapy

| Effect | Mechanism | Prevalence | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rebound effect** | Auditory neurons resume hyperactive firing after sound offset; contrast effect makes tinnitus seem louder | Common (~80% experience some RI, rebound follows) | Seconds to minutes |
| **Residual excitation** | Sound exposure temporarily *increases* rather than suppresses tinnitus loudness | Rare (documented in Sedley et al. 2012) | Typically < 1 minute |
| **New/changed tinnitus tones** | Reported in NMT adverse reaction data — mechanism unclear, possibly related to central gain recalibration | ~32% in one NMT study (12/37 participants) | Transient in most cases |
| **Post-suppression neural rebound** | ~40% of auditory neurons show increased firing above baseline after sound offset (Galazyuk et al., animal data) | Common at neuronal level | 5–42 seconds per neuron |

### Reactive Tinnitus

Reactive tinnitus is a subtype where the tinnitus reacts (increases/changes) in response to external sound. For these patients:
- Sound therapy can worsen symptoms if introduced too aggressively
- Clinical guidance: stabilize sound sensitivity before starting sustained therapy
- Lower volume and shorter sessions are essential
- If symptoms consistently worsen, discontinue and seek clinical evaluation

### Contraindications for Self-Directed TMNMT

- Pulsatile tinnitus (requires medical evaluation for vascular causes)
- Sudden-onset tinnitus (< 3 months — may resolve spontaneously, warrants ENT evaluation)
- Reactive tinnitus or significant hyperacusis (requires clinical supervision)
- Noise-type tinnitus (notched therapy is ineffective; broadband masking only)

### Key Safety Principle

Clinical TMNMT studies used moderate volumes and gradual protocols. A 2022 RCT found that "excessive compensation for hearing loss frequency worsened tinnitus both clinically and electrophysiologically." More is not better — conservative settings are closer to the studied protocols.

---

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **Tinnitus is a brain disorder**, not just an ear problem — the phantom sound is generated centrally.
2. **No cure exists** as of 2026; management focuses on habituation and quality-of-life improvement.
3. **CBT has the strongest evidence** for long-term distress reduction.
4. **Notched therapies** (noise and music) are the most mechanistically justified sound treatments.
5. **Bimodal stimulation (Lenire)** is the most significant new treatment in a decade — FDA cleared 2023.
6. **Hearing aids** remain the most impactful intervention when hearing loss is the driver.
7. **Prevention** (hearing protection, volume limits) is far more effective than any current treatment.
